Amazon geography

The Amazon Region comprises 42% of the national territory and is known as the lung of the world due to its vast expanse of jungle. It is home to the largest variety of freshwater fish on the planet and a variety of exotic mammals such as the pink river dolphin, manatee, jaguar, cougar, giant otter, peccary, tapir, capybara, sloth and various primates. The world's largest river turtle and alligator are also found there. It is made up of forests of all kinds, with an incredible number of trees, such as Itahuba, Caricari, Tajibos, Cedro, Cuta barcina, Almandrillo and others. In addition, the best medicinal plants capable of curing all kinds of diseases grow there. It is a paradise full of magic, with wild flowers of peculiar beauty and great color.

The Colombian Amazon is made up of the Andes mountain range and plains that extend eastward to the borders with Brazil, Peru, Ecuador and Venezuela. Sharing with these countries the Amazon Rainforest, which has one of the greatest biodiversity on the planet. The second longest and mightiest river on the planet, the Amazon River, runs through the Amazon. Which contains more water than the Nile, the Yangtze and the Mississippi combined. This river also has the largest hydrographic basin in the world, around 7.05 million square kilometers, being one fifth of the world's river flow. The basins located in Colombia that drain into the Amazon River are the Caquetá and Putumayo rivers. Also within the most important rivers are the Guaviare, Apaporis and Vaupés.

Indigenous people of the Amazon

“Indigenous peoples are living libraries. They are the guardians, caretakers and gardeners of the Amazon and the Planet. Every time an indigenous people is exterminated and disappears, a face of Tupãna (God) dies, the cosmos, the planet and all of humanity are impoverished ”. (Wise Bernardo Alves, from the Sateré-Mawé indigenous people). The disappearance of a culture can be as serious or more serious than the disappearance of an animal or plant species. The imposition of a hegemonic style of life linked to a mode of production can be as harmful as the alteration of ecosystems.

Puerto Córdoba and Comeyafú have been established as Indigenous Reservations in September 1985. In the case of Camaritagua, it was established in 2002. Together they are home to an important cultural diversity: thirteen indigenous peoples live there, including the Bora ethnic group, Barasano, Carijona, Cubeo, Letuama, Miraña, Matapi, Macuna, Tatuyo, Tariano, Tanimuca, Uitoto and Yucuna.

The island of the Micos

The island of the Micos or Arara Island is a fluvial island of the Amazon River belonging to Colombia, located in the municipality of Leticia, southeast of the department of Amazonas, near the border with Peru. The island is about 20 kilometers upriver from the city of Leticia, at the height of the town of Santa Sofía. The island has a size of around 450 hectares. American Mike Tsalikys established a station there in the 1960s to raise monkeys and other living things. The animals were mainly sold for experimental purposes, but animal experiments were also conducted on site.


Mike Tsalikys' company had to cease operations in the 1990s. Since then, the island has become a tourist attraction. Leticia offers boat trips with a travel time of around one hour. Today there are several thousand monkeys on the island, mostly squirrel monkeys. At the north end of the island there is a visitor station with mooring facilities. The keepers there have used the animals for visiting groups.
